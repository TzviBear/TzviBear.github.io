Basic Info

Sever's Disease is the most common cause of heel pain among children, especially those of them who play sports or exercise regurlarly. Also known as calcaneal apophysitis, Sever’s disease occurs when the growth plate in the back of the heel becomes inflamed and painful. It is caused by continuated stress on the growth plate at the heel bone, at the site where the Achilles tendon pulls on the back of the heel. This growth plate serves as the attachment point for the Achilles tendon, which is a strong band of tissue that connects the calf muscles at the back of the leg to the heel bone. It typically occurs during periods of rapid growth. This diagram explains the affected area of Sever's Disease.

Causes

The growth plate in the back of the heel is made of bone cartilage. The Achilles tendon connects the calf muscles to the growth plate, and because bones grow faster than muscle, especially in the foot, the Achilles tendon tightens at certain points in the physical development of a young kid.

Activities that are most likely to cause this include:

  • sports that involve running and jumping on hard surfaces (track, basketball, gymnastics)
  • sports that involve wearing cleats (football, lacrosse, soccer)
  • overtraining of young athletes
  • shoes that don't have adequate support

Victims

Sever’s disease most often affects children during growth spurts. Sever’s disease is rare in older teens. By late adolescence, a child’s growth plates have hardened into solid bone, and their muscles and tendons have lengthened to match the growth of their bones. In boys, growth spurts happen when they’re between 10 and 15 years old. In girls, growth spurts typically occur between the ages of 8 and 13. Athletes who participate in high-impact sports are at higher risk of developing Sever’s disease. Less active kids can also be affected, though.

Symptoms

The most common symptom of Sever's Disease is pain in the back of the heel, which can extend to the bottom and sides of the heel.

Symptoms include:

  • heel pain with limping, especially after running
  • difficulty walking around
  • swelling and redness around the heel area
  • foot stiffness, discomfort or pain after sleeping
  • achiness throught the heel

Diagnosis and Treatment

From the author's personal experience, diagnosis can come from a pediatrician (foot doctor) or a family doctor. Sever's Disease is usually easy to spot and diagnose. If you want to try to diagnose at home, you can squeeze your child's foot at different points to see where the pain is. Doctors will usually do this as well as x-rays and watching them walk, hop or jump to come up with a complete diagnosis.

Treatments include:

  • physical therapy to stretch the calf muscle and strengthen the leg muscles and tendons
  • Over-the-counter medication like ibuprofen, tylenol or motrin
  • shoes with an elevated heel
  • heel gel pads/inserts to cushion the heel and relieve stress from the heel and ankle
  • stretching exercises for the Achilles Tendon that can reduce stress and relieve pain

Outcome

It is not unusual for Sever’s disease to reoccur. This typically happens when a child once again increases sports activities or does not do the proper treatment methods. Wearing shoes that provide good support to the foot and heel may help prevent recurrence. Sever’s disease will not return once a child is fully grown and the growth plate in the heel has completely transformed from cartilage into solid bone.
